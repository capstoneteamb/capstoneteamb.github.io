Capstone Management Webapp - Overview

A web app designed to help with the PSU Capstone classes. At minimum, it will help with the 360 review process. At best, it will help with Capstone team lead and team management as well. The web app is to be hosted on a a stock Linux installation using Apache2 or Nginx, will use Postgres as the database, and will be written in Python. Security is a high priority, and must be built in from the ground up.

Updates

Sprint 1: 3/15/2019 to 4/16/2019 (spring break not included)

In our first sprint, we set out to accomplish a first round of user stories (which encompassed over half of the minimum viable product). When the original end date of the sprint came, we weren't finished. We had some team members who had their individual stories’ tasks completed, but the project was not in one cohesive application. At that point we realized that we had not taken the necessary time to finalize the overall organization of the project, so extra work would be need to be done to merge the changes for all of the user stories we were working on. With some additional security research during the sprint and subsequent discussion, we decided to implement the SQLAlchemy library into our code as well. We then point extended our sprint by one week.

As the new deadline came, we found that we still weren’t done. SQLAlchemy was proving difficult to incorporate into our code, require significant re-development of our code. We then reflected on our sprint, and on some of the things we could do better. Chief among these were being more communicative with one another and spending more time planning our organization as a team. Instead of ending the sprint there, we decided to extend it to our next meeting and focus our efforts on trying to get our code conpleted (without SQLAlchemy--that part could wait) and merged so we would have something to present to our sponsor (because we arranged a demonstration for that day).

When that day came, we still weren’t finished, but proceeded to demonstrate what functionality our application had (on the main and on some of the development branches of our GitHub repo). Even as someone familiar with the Capstone process, our sponsor seemed to be very pleased with the amount of work we had been able to accomplish. While he stated that there were changes he wished to have made, he stressed that these were minor in comparison to our achievement thus far. After finishing, we extended our sprint one more time (officially making it four weeks long).

At the final end of the sprint, some of us had accomplished all, or the majority of the tasks we thought we needed to do at the time to complete our user stories, some changes simply waiting for peer review (which we made required if we wanted the changes to make it into the main repository). We still couldn’t call anything done however, because we decided that we would only call a user story done once it made its way into the master branch in its finalized state.

There was one user story we weren’t close to finishing, however: one about setting our log-in system up, but there were external factors were at play. Namely, the cooperation of other people. Initially, we decided to use the ODIN login system, and thus relied on the cooperation of OIT. While we received an initial, affirmative response from them, they stopped responding to our emails. We ended up giving up on ODIN and shot for the CAT supported CAS log in system instead. They also responded in the affirmative, and thankfully, continued communicating with us. They recently provided us the information we will need to implement the log in system as well. Even with the information, we were still left with about a sprint’s worth of time to work on it, though I don’t think anyone jumped on the information and started working on it when everyone in the team became aware of it (misconception, miscommunication, lack of communication, or lack of proper team management, could have been a part of this). It might also be worth mentioning that in order to use the CAT’s CAS system, we needed the CAT to finish setting up a virtual machine for us (one that will host our application in the foreseeable future), and that only got set up recently (we could have, of course, tried setting up our own CAS server, but even if we did, it wouldn’t have been a permanent solution and we may not have marked the user story as completed anyway).

Overall, while we didn't officially finish any of our user stories, we still might have gotten a decent amount of work done (especially if the witness of our sponsor means anything). It might also be fair to say that if SQLAlchemy hadn't given us as much grief as it did, we might have been at least a little but further along. In the future, we might consider more conservative planning, and we will aim to avoid four week sprints. Thank you for your time!
